## ASSUMPTIONS SHEET JUDGMENT

### When to show a dollar amount vs. say "included"

The core question is: do you want the network's eyes on this number or not?

**Show the number when:**
- It's a number so large the network needs to see it and explicitly agree to it. If they find it later and didn't know, that's a problem. Better to surface it.
- It tells a useful story. "Set Construction — $400,000" and "LED Screens — $200,000" feels better than "Set — $600,000" even though the total is the same. Breaking it out makes each number feel more reasonable.
- It's a key creative decision that has a specific cost attached — like prank allowances, challenge allowances, or prize money that's inside the budget.

**Say "included" when:**
- The number could invite an argument or a cut you don't want to have. If lighting costs $180,000 a week and you just say "lighting included," nobody fights about it. If you write $180,000/wk, someone's going to ask why.
- It's a standard cost that every production has and nobody questions. Music library. Rigging. Basic location fees. Just say included.
- The number is high enough to look bad out of context but is completely reasonable for the type of production. "Included" protects you.
- You're already over budget on something and you don't want to draw attention to a specific line.

**The animal wrangling example (April 9, 2026):**
- Animals at $210,000 — probably fine to list with the number. It's not outrageous, and it tells the network the animals are there and accounted for.
- Animals at $800,000 — say "animal wrangling and coordination — included." That's a big number and you don't want a negotiation about it. They can find it in the budget if they go looking.
- Animals at $1,500,000+ — you might actually want to show it, because at that level the network needs to consciously agree to it. Hiding a $1.5M line and having them find it later is worse than the conversation upfront.
- The rule: medium-high numbers that could look bad out of context — hide them. Very high numbers where network sign-off matters — show them.

### What belongs in Overall vs. other sections

Overall Show Specifics is for things a line producer or production manager would walk into your office and tell you — things that aren't in the numbers but define how the show works. Format decisions. Talent structure. Creative choices that drove budget decisions. Things the network needs to know before they read a single number.

If it's in the budget as a line item, it goes in the relevant section. If it's context for understanding the budget, it goes in Overall.

### On calling out things that are outside the budget

Always flag outside-budget items clearly. The network needs to know what they're signing up for beyond the number on the page. Talent and prize money are the most common outside-budget items. Don't bury them or imply they're included when they're not.

### On fees

If you know the percentage, show it. Networks expect to see Prod Co Fee at 10%, legal at 1.5%, agency at 3%. If you just say "included" on fees without percentages, it looks like you're hiding something. Fees are the one place where transparency is always better.

## TALENT JUDGMENT

### Host fees and the "outside budget" decision

Host fees are almost always outside the budget during the development and early production phase because:
1. You often don't know who the host is yet
2. Even if you do, the deal isn't closed
3. The number can be enormous and you don't want it in the budget total affecting your per-episode cost

Once a host is locked and the deal is done, sometimes it gets folded in, sometimes it stays outside. Depends on the network relationship and what makes the per-episode number look better.

### Contestant costs

Background checks, psych evaluations, and medical are always worth calling out explicitly because networks care about liability. If they're in the budget, say so. It signals professionalism and due diligence.

Contestant stipends — always include the amount. It's usually small and it's a contractual obligation the network needs to know about.

## GENERAL PRODUCTION JUDGMENT

### The "walking into my office" test

If a line producer or PM walked into Marc's office and asked whether to include something, Marc would ask:
1. Is it in the budget? (If yes, it probably belongs somewhere in the assumptions)
2. Is it a number the network needs to consciously agree to? (If yes, show it)
3. Could it invite an argument that wastes everyone's time? (If yes, say included)
4. Does it define something important about how this show works? (If yes, it goes in Overall)
5. Is it just standard production infrastructure that every show has? (If yes, say included and move on)

### On note turnaround schedules

Always include note turnaround tiers when you have them, because they're a contractual commitment to the network. RC, FC, LC — the network holds you to these. If you put them in the assumptions and miss them, that's a conversation. But leaving them out is worse because then there's no agreed standard at all.

### On post weeks

Post weeks are one of the most negotiated items between production and network. Always be precise when you can. A vague post schedule invites the network to compress it. A specific number — "12 weeks offline per episode" — is much harder to argue with.

## THE "EMAIL MARC" CONCEPT (added April 9, 2026)

During the founding period of StudioChief, when users hit a genuinely ambiguous production judgment call that the AI isn't confident about, the ideal path is to surface the question to Marc directly. Marc answers, the answer gets captured here, and the tool gets smarter.

This is essentially Marc sitting in his office answering the same questions he's answered for 30 years — except now the answers become permanent product knowledge instead of disappearing when the conversation ends.

Questions worth escalating to Marc:
- Edge cases where the "right" answer genuinely depends on context the AI can't see
- New production formats or budget structures the AI hasn't encountered
- Situations where the standard rules would produce a clearly wrong result
- Anything where Marc's network relationships or industry knowledge would change the answer

Questions NOT worth escalating:
- Anything clearly covered by existing rules
- Standard formatting decisions
- Math
